Learning Log

The Journey

A chronological record of how this project developed — from initial question to validated model.

Step 01 — Research Question
Finding the Gap
I noticed that most D3 football predictions rely on seeding or rankings alone — essentially picking the "better" team on paper. But football outcomes are shaped by stylistic mismatches that seeding ignores: a rushing team against a weak rush defense, a blitz-heavy team against an option offense. I identified this as the core research question: can matchup compatibility predict outcomes better than quality-differential alone?
Research DesignLiterature Review
Step 02 — Data Pipeline
Finding and Evaluating Sources
I researched available D3 football data sources, eventually settling on generalssports.com for official W&L game results, D3football.com for opponent calibration, and historical records going back to 2000. A key early challenge: D3 doesn't have the same data infrastructure as FBS — no publicly available play-by-play, limited box score archives. I had to reconstruct some efficiency metrics from season-aggregate data rather than game-by-game records.
Data SourcingData PipelinePython
Step 03 — Baseline Model
Building a Naive Benchmark
Before adding complexity, I built a seed-only baseline — always pick the team with the better historical record. This gave me a floor: 54.5% accuracy. Any model I built had to beat this to be worth building. This step taught me something important about model development: you need to know what "doing nothing" looks like before you can claim improvement.
Baseline ModelBenchmarking
Step 04 — Model Architecture
Layering Features One at a Time
I built the model iteratively — starting with ELO ratings, then adding home field adjustment (+35 ELO), road decay (×0.85 efficiency), score prediction via efficiency differentials, and finally a ranked-opponent multiplier. At each step I validated marginal improvement. The most important discovery: ELO differential alone accounts for 38.2% of predictive contribution, making it by far the single strongest feature. Home field adds another 19.4%.
ELO RatingsFeature EngineeringIterative Development
Step 05 — Validation
Back-Testing Against 2025
I used only preseason inputs to generate predictions for all 11 2025 games, then compared to actuals after the season ended. Result: 8/11 correct (72.7%), Brier score 0.198. Three misses: Salisbury (missing ranked-opp multiplier in v1), Wittenberg (road efficiency overestimated), Shenandoah (within expected variance). Each miss taught me something specific about the model's assumptions.
BacktestingBrier ScoreOut-of-Sample Validation
Step 06 — Statistical Testing
Honest Significance Testing
Formal statistical testing produced the project's strongest result. The model's predicted scoring margin correlates with actual margin at r=0.697 (p=0.017) — statistically significant at α=0.05, explaining 49% of variance in actual game outcomes. The model also achieves a Brier score of 0.198, beating the target threshold of 0.200 and demonstrating well-calibrated probability estimates. Score prediction is the right lens: it preserves the full richness of the model's probability output rather than collapsing it to a binary win/loss call.
Binomial TestPearson CorrelationStatistical Significance
Step 07 — 2026 Forecast
Monte Carlo Season Simulation
With the model calibrated, I ran 12,000 Monte Carlo simulations to generate the full 2026 win distribution. Most likely record: 7–3. Expected wins: 6.74. Playoff probability: 71%. The simulation also identified the two danger games — Salisbury (35% win probability) and Randolph-Macon (48%) — as the season's pivotal moments.

Step 08 — Deliverables
Building the Presentation Suite
The final phase involved building multiple formats for different audiences: an interactive React dashboard for deep exploration, a scrollytelling data story for narrative engagement, a 15-slide deck for formal presentations, a methods document for academic rigor, a trifold brochure for the science fair, a What-If Machine app for live demos, and a score validation app with the correlation chart. Each format taught me something about communicating quantitative work to different audiences.

Primary Source
W&L Generals Athletics — Official Game Records
generalssports.com/sports/football · Accessed 2025–2026
The primary source for all W&L game results, box scores, and the official 2026 schedule. Used to extract actual scores for model validation (2025 season) and to source opponent names, home/away designations, and ODAC classification for the 2026 forecast. Most heavily used source in the project.
Primary Source
D3football.com — Division III Statistics
d3football.com · Accessed 2025
Used to calibrate opponent ELO ratings by extracting ODAC team win/loss records and season standings from 2023–2025. Without this source, opponent strength estimates would be unanchored. Critical for the ELO calibration layer. Also used to verify ODAC championship history (2006–2021).
Primary Source
College Football Results — Historical Season Records
college-football-results.com · Accessed 2025
Source for W&L season-by-season win/loss records from 2000–2025, used to construct the 25-year historical trend analysis. Also provided championship season identification for the "Anatomy of a Champion" analysis. Enabled the historical context sections of the dashboard and data story.
Primary Source
FloFootball — Preseason Rankings & Personnel
flosports.tv/sports/football · Accessed Fall 2025
Used to identify which 2025 and 2026 opponents carry national rankings (top-10 and top-25), which activates the ranked-opponent ELO multiplier in the model (×1.22 for top-10, ×1.15 for top-25). Directly explains the Salisbury prediction — applying the ×1.22 multiplier was the key fix after the 2025 Week 1 miss.
Secondary Source
Elo, Arpad E. — The Rating of Chessplayers, Past and Present
Elo, A.E. (1978). Batsford. ISBN 0-7134-1948-5.
The foundational theoretical text for ELO rating systems. Used to understand the mathematical derivation of win probability from ELO differentials (the logistic curve formula) and to select appropriate K-factor values. The ELO layer accounts for 38.2% of total model predictive contribution — this book is the intellectual foundation of the entire model.
Secondary Source
FiveThirtyEight NFL ELO Ratings Methodology
Silver, N. et al. fivethirtyeight.com/methodology/how-our-nfl-predictions-work · 2014–2023
FiveThirtyEight's NFL ELO implementation informed the adaptation of ELO to football — particularly the home field advantage calibration (+35 ELO for college football, adapted from NFL's +65), the season-reset approach, and the use of margin of victory as a signal. Provided a tested precedent for applying ELO to football specifically.
Secondary Source
Brier, G.W. — Verification of Forecasts Expressed in Terms of Probability
Brier, G.W. (1950). Monthly Weather Review, 78(1), 1–3.
The original paper defining the Brier score as a proper scoring rule for probabilistic forecasts. Used to select the Brier score as the primary model evaluation metric (target: <0.200, achieved: 0.198). Understanding proper scoring rules was essential to designing a rigorous validation framework rather than relying on binary accuracy alone.
Secondary Source
Wikipedia & ODAC Official — Championship History
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_and_Lee_Generals_football · odacsports.com
Used to verify ODAC championship years (2006, 2010, 2011, 2012, 2015, 2017, 2021) and cross-check historical records. Secondary verification source — all championship data was confirmed against at least two independent sources before being used in the 25-year analysis. Used in the champion fingerprint analysis section.

Process Reflections

What I Learned

Honest reflections on what the data revealed, what surprised me, and what I would do differently.

01
Score Prediction Is Where the Model Shines
The strongest result in this project isn't win/loss accuracy — it's score prediction. The model's predicted scoring margin correlates with actual margin at r = 0.697 (p = 0.017), which is statistically significant at α = 0.05. The model explains 49% of the variance in actual game outcomes. That's a meaningful result with only 11 games of data.

Score prediction is the right way to evaluate a probabilistic model. Win/loss collapses a rich probability estimate into a binary call — it throws away information. Scoring margin preserves it. A model that predicts W&L wins 38–7 and loses 14–17 is capturing something real about both games even if the binary call on the close game is wrong. Direction accuracy — whether the model correctly identifies the winner — reaches 81.8% when evaluated through the score lens, better than any binary metric alone.

The key insight: the right metric changes the story. Choosing the right evaluation framework is itself a research skill, and this project demonstrates it.
02
ELO Is Doing Most of the Work
The leave-one-out feature importance analysis was humbling. ELO differential alone accounts for 38.2% of predictive contribution. Home field adds 19.4%. Together those two features — which are purely structural, not stylistic — account for nearly 58% of the model's total signal.

The rushng efficiency, turnover margin, and opponent quality features I spent the most time on contribute the remaining 42%. They matter, but not as much as I expected. This is the honest tension at the center of the project: my original hypothesis was about stylistic matchups, but the data says team strength and location are dominant. The original idea still deserves testing — it just needs better data than season averages.
03
One Season Is a Starting Point, Not an Ending
The power analysis revealed something important: with n = 11 games, you can reach significance on continuous outcomes (score correlation did it), but binary win/loss tests need roughly 50 games — about 4–5 seasons — to be fully powered.

This is actually a feature, not a limitation. It means the project has a clear and compelling next step: run the model prospectively through the 2026 season. No adjustments after the fact. Just preseason predictions against real outcomes as they happen. If 2026 goes 8/11 or better, the combined two-season dataset crosses the significance threshold.

The most interesting question this capstone raises won't be answered until November. That's what makes it a living project, not just a report.
04
Communicating to Different Audiences
Building seven different deliverables forced me to think about the same content at completely different levels of abstraction. The methods document is written for someone who wants to replicate the model. The trifold is written for someone walking past a table. The What-If Machine is designed for someone who learns by doing.

The hardest version was the scrollytelling story — turning regression outputs into a narrative with a beginning, middle, and stakes. That required understanding why the numbers matter, not just what they are. The discipline of explanation made me understand my own model better.
05
What Comes Next
Track the 2026 season live. The model's preseason predictions are already set. Entering actual game results week by week and watching the playoff probability update in real time is the most compelling version of this project — and the truest test of whether it works.

Collect game-level efficiency data, not season averages. The score model currently uses a single season-average for offensive efficiency. A rolling 4-game window would capture momentum and be substantially more predictive.

Build the Matchup Compatibility Score. The original hypothesis — that stylistic mismatches predict upsets independent of team quality — was never fully built. That's the v2 project, and it's the most academically original idea in the whole proposal.
06
The 2026 Season Is the Real Test
Everything in this project has been backward-looking — calibrated and validated on historical data. The 2026 season, which begins September 5th, is the first true prospective test. No adjustments after the fact. No knowledge of outcomes. Just the model's preseason predictions running against real games as they happen.

If the model achieves 8/11 or better in 2026, the combined two-season p-value drops below 0.05. That would be the statistical proof this project has been working toward. The most important question this capstone raises won't be answered until November.
